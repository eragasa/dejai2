# [[stylizedfact.00|Stylized Facts — Definition, Use, and Representation]]

---

## [[stylizedfact.01.00|1. Definition of a Stylized Fact]]

### [[stylizedfacto.01.01|1.1 Working definition]]
A **stylized fact** is a deliberately simplified statement that captures a recurring pattern in the observed behavior of a system.  
It abstracts away detail in order to preserve what appears stable, salient, and repeatedly encountered.

### [[stylizedfacto.01.02|1.2 Epistemic status]]
Stylized facts are empirical in origin but not formal empirical results.  
They are neither laws nor hypotheses, but intermediate descriptive commitments.

### [[stylizedfacto.01.03|1.3 Level of correctness]]
Stylized facts assert **qualitative correctness** rather than quantitative accuracy.  
They characterize how a system behaves in broad terms, not exact magnitudes or functional forms.

### [[stylizedfacto.01.04|1.4 Purpose]]
Stylized facts exist to:
- stabilize reasoning in the presence of noise, complexity, or incomplete theory,
- provide shared descriptive anchors prior to formal modeling or specification.

---

## [[stylizedfact.02|2. Stylized Facts Across Domains]]

### [[stylizedfact.02.01|2.1 Economics and social science]]
In economics and related fields, stylized facts:
- summarize persistent empirical patterns across datasets, time periods, or populations,
- function as constraints on acceptable models rather than propositions to be proven,
- are typically expressed qualitatively, sometimes accompanied by illustrative data.

### [[stylizedfact.02.02|2.2 Natural and physical sciences]]
In the natural sciences, stylized facts:
- often precede formal laws or mechanistic explanations,
- arise from repeated experimental or observational experience,
- are usually scoped to specific regimes or conditions.

### [[stylizedfact.02.03|2.3 Engineering and computer systems]]
In engineering and systems work, stylized facts:
- capture recurring behaviors, trade-offs, or failure modes observed across implementations,
- are grounded primarily in repeated practical experience rather than controlled experiments,
- guide design decisions under uncertainty.

### [[stylizedfact.02.04|2.4 Qualitative and interpretive fields]]
In qualitative and interpretive domains, stylized facts:
- appear as named patterns or motifs recurring across texts, cases, or histories,
- emphasize recognizability and interpretive usefulness rather than measurement.

### [[stylizedfact.02.05|2.5 Common thread]]
Across domains, stylized facts fix a **descriptive stance**: a way of talking about how a system tends to behave.  
Differences between fields lie in evidentiary standards, not in the core concept.

---

## [[stylizedfacto.03.00|3. Stylized Facts as Textual Artifacts]]

### [[stylizedfacto.03.01|3.1 Natural language as the primary medium]]
Stylized facts operate at a level where formalization may be premature or inappropriate.  
Natural language allows abstraction, revision, and contextual nuance.

### [[stylizedfacto.03.02|3.2 Separation of observation and interpretation]]
Many practices implicitly or explicitly distinguish between:
- the observed recurring pattern,
- how it manifests in practice,
- the interpretation or takeaway drawn from it.

Maintaining this separation supports later revision without erasing historical context.

### [[stylizedfacto.03.03|3.3 Durability and revision]]
Stylized facts are intended to persist longer than individual notes or observations.  
They may be refined, weakened, or discarded over time without implying that earlier usage was erroneous.

---

## [[stylizedfacto.04.00|4. Domain-Specific Formats and Conventions]]

### [[stylizedfacto.04.01|4.1 Narrative formats]]
Common in economics and qualitative research.  
Stylized facts are expressed as short paragraphs or enumerated lists embedded in prose.

### [[stylizedfacto.04.02|4.2 Structured textual formats]]
Common in engineering and systems work.  
Stylized facts are broken into labeled components (e.g., observation, manifestation, implication).

### [[stylizedfacto.04.03|4.3 Use of identifiers and labels]]
Many domains adopt informal identifiers or short names to enable reference and discussion.  
Stability of reference is often more important than formal naming schemes.

### [[stylizedfacto.04.04|4.4 Absence of a canonical format]]
No single representation of stylized facts is standard across domains.  
Format reflects local practice, audience, and intended use.

---

## [[stylizedfacto.05.00|5. Motivation for a General Schema]]

### [[stylizedfacto.05.01|5.1 Why representation matters]]
Stylized facts are often scattered across prose, making them difficult to:
- reference consistently,
- compare across contexts,
- revise without loss of history.

A schema stabilizes them as identifiable artifacts.

### [[stylizedfacto.05.02|5.2 Constraints on generality]]
A general schema must:
- avoid imposing domain-specific methodological commitments,
- preserve the textual and interpretive nature of stylized facts.

### [[stylizedfacto.05.03|5.3 Design goals]]
The guiding goals of a general schema are:
- minimal structure,
- text-first representation,
- durability over time,
- capacity for later specialization.

---

## [[stylizedfacto.06.00|6. The Most General Form of a Stylized-Fact Schema]]

### [[stylizedfacto.06.01|6.1 Stylized fact as an identifiable artifact]]
At its most general level, a stylized fact is a **named unit of text** that can be referenced, revised, and discussed independently.

### [[stylizedfacto.06.02|6.2 Core conceptual components]]
A minimal conceptual schema distinguishes between:

- **Identity**  
  A stable handle or identifier that allows the fact to be referenced over time.

- **Descriptive content**  
  Text that records:
  - the recurring pattern being observed,
  - how it tends to appear in practice.

- **Interpretive content**  
  Text that records:
  - the current understanding, implication, or takeaway drawn from the observation.

These components may be combined in prose, but they are conceptually distinct.

### [[stylizedfacto.06.03|6.3 What the general schema does not require]]
The general schema does not require:
- numerical measurements,
- formal hypotheses,
- causal mechanisms,
- explicit falsification criteria,
- universal scope.

### [[stylizedfacto.06.04|6.4 Textual primacy]]
All components of the schema are fundamentally textual.  
Any later formalization (metrics, tests, models) is secondary and optional.

### [[stylizedfacto.06.05|6.5 Adaptability to domain-specific formats]]
- Narrative formats (Section 4.1) typically collapse descriptive and interpretive content into prose.
- Structured formats (Section 4.2) make these components explicit via labeled fields.
- Identifier usage (Section 4.3) maps naturally onto the identity component.

The general schema is therefore a **conceptual scaffold**, not a fixed data structure.

---

## [[stylizedfacto.07.00|7. Specialization and Extension]]

### [[stylizedfacto.07.01|7.1 Domain-driven specialization]]
Domains may extend the general schema by adding fields such as scope, evidence, or references.  
Extensions refine interpretation without redefining the core concept.

### [[stylizedfacto.07.02|7.2 Layering rather than replacement]]
Specialized schemas should preserve the general form as a base layer.  
This allows stylized facts to remain intelligible across contexts and over time.

---

## [[stylizedfacto.08.00|8. Summary]]
Stylized facts are simplified textual descriptions of recurring system behavior.  
They operate at a qualitative level, fixing descriptive style rather than formal specification.  
A general schema identifies their minimal conceptual components without enforcing domain-specific rigor.  
Specialization can occur later, guided by use rather than imposed in advance.
